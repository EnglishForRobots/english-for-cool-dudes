💡 Did you know? Courts set a very high bar for MAC clauses. A buyer who invokes one too casually — for general market jitters rather than a genuinely serious, unforeseen event — often loses in court, and still owes a break fee on top. "I've changed my mind" is never a valid MAC event.

Definitions
ARequirements that must be met before a deal can legally complete
BLets a buyer walk away after a serious, unforeseen event
CA payment owed if a party walks away without valid legal reason
DThe buyer's investigation into a company before agreeing to buy it
EExtra payment made later if the company hits performance targets
FA clause where one party compensates the other for a specific named loss

StatementT  /  F

2. Due diligence normally happens before a deal is signed, not after.
3. An NDA can be legally enforced even if the deal ultimately falls through.
4. Earn-outs mean the seller receives 100% of the purchase price immediately at closing.
5. Closing conditions are simply a formality and never actually stop a deal from happening.
6. A break fee is designed to discourage a party from abandoning a deal without good reason.

E — Discussion: Your turn
Use the vocabulary from today. Write notes or discuss with a partner.
Many buyers assume a MAC clause is a "get-out-of-jail-free card" whenever a deal starts to look less attractive. Why do courts set such a high bar for using one — and how would you explain this to a client considering walking away?
Earn-outs can create tension: the buyer controls the business, but the seller's payment depends on its performance. What kind of clause protects a seller against a buyer who might sabotage the numbers on purpose?
A signed contract creates binding obligations, but closing conditions can still stop a deal completing. Can you think of a closing condition that might reasonably cause a well-negotiated deal to fail anyway?
